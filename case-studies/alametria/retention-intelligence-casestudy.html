The Challenge

The Number That
Told Nothing

The retention report read 87%. Leadership loved it. The marketing lead didn't. He'd been asked to design a targeted retention campaign — but the moment he looked past that headline number, he found himself staring at a black box.

Every repeat purchase was treated identically, regardless of when it happened or why. A trader who bought five challenge accounts in their first week. A trader returning after six months of silence. A trader buying a new account every month like clockwork after each withdrawal. The system called all three "retained" and moved on.

"I found three completely different traders all labeled as retained. Ahmed bought 5 challenge accounts in his first week. Maria came back after 6 months of silence. And Chen buys a new account every month after each withdrawal like clockwork. How can I send them all the same email?"

Marketing Lead · Prop Trading Platform

This wasn't just a frustration — it was a structural business problem. Without behavioral segmentation, the platform was spending its retention budget on the wrong people, sending the wrong messages, and missing the signals that could have told them which customers were about to leave and which were actually thriving.

One Label for Everything

Every repeat purchase — 2 days or 200 days after the last — carried the same "retained" tag with no behavioral context attached.

Wrong Messages, Wrong People

Stackers, funded winners, and genuinely dormant traders were all receiving the same campaign email. Targeting didn't exist.

3 Days for One Segmentation Answer

Without a unified model, answering a basic segment question required 3 days of manual record-checking across multiple data sources.

Decisions Driven by Assumption

Marketing strategy was based on intuition. No timing intelligence. No behavioral triggers. No way to quantify what "good retention" even looked like.

Primary Risk: A growing platform was making expensive, high-volume marketing decisions without ever truly understanding the behavioral reality of its customer base. The 87% retention figure was accurate — but it was a single average describing ten completely different situations.
02
Methodology

How the Framework
Was Built

The solution began not with a new tool, but with a new question. Instead of asking "did this customer buy again?" the framework asked "where is this customer in their lifecycle right now?"

Every purchase was enriched with trader identity data, geographic region, product details, and — critically — trading activity metrics. This last element is what separates the framework from a simple date-gap model. Purchase timing alone is incomplete. What a customer was doing between purchases tells the real story.

Data Sources

The framework pulls from four primary data layers, joined at the individual trader level:

  • Revenue Records — Every payment with date, amount, product, and coupon usage
  • Trader Profiles — Identity, country, region, account login
  • Trading Activity — Daily active trading days, last active date
  • Region Mapping — Country-to-region classification (Gulf, Europe, Asia, Americas)

Critical Insight

Not All Long Gaps
Are the Same

The most consequential discovery in the framework wasn't a number. It was a distinction that had been invisible to the platform since it launched.

Two traders can both show a purchase gap of over six months. On the surface, they look identical. The framework separates them with a single question: was this trader still actively trading during that gap?

Late Retention
Still Winning
365+ days since last purchase · still actively trading

This trader hasn't needed to buy a new challenge account because they're already funded and trading profitably. They are the platform's best outcome — not a churn risk. Sending a "we miss you" email to this person is not just wasteful, it's actively damaging to the relationship.

Do not disturb. This is success.
Reacquisition
Truly Gone. Now Back.
181+ days · no purchases AND no trading activity

This trader stopped buying and stopped trading entirely. They were completely disengaged. Then something — a campaign, a new product, a referral, a timely promotion — brought them back. Every single Reacquisition is a proof point for whatever triggered the return.

Study every one of these. They hold marketing gold.
The real-world consequence: Before the framework, the marketing team had prepared a win-back email campaign targeting traders with 90+ day purchase gaps. Had it launched, it would have landed in the inboxes of hundreds of funded traders who were actively profitable — people who had no idea they were being treated as "lost." The framework identified and excluded this group before the campaign sent. Complaint rate later dropped 40% after this correction was applied systematically.

Finding 1: Stacking Is the Strategy, Not the Anomaly

Before the framework, traders who purchased five accounts in their first week were flagged as unusual. After segmentation, the picture inverted entirely: 83% of new traders exhibit Acquisition Burst behavior.

Stacking is a rational strategy. Traders run multiple challenge accounts simultaneously to increase their statistical odds of passing at least one. Understanding this changed how the platform onboarded and communicated with new customers entirely.

Framework Scalability

Built Once.
Compounds Forever.

The Retention Intelligence framework was designed as infrastructure, not a report. It runs continuously, classifying every purchase the moment it occurs. There is no human intervention required after the initial build — the system learns from the business as it grows.

The compounding value is structural: each month of classified data makes behavioral patterns clearer. Each campaign tested against a specific segment produces signal that improves the next campaign. Each Reacquisition studied reduces the cost of future win-back efforts. The framework gets more valuable the longer it runs.

The core logic is also product-agnostic. While this implementation was built for a prop trading platform, the lifecycle model applies directly to any transaction-driven business where customer return timing and activity between purchases carry behavioral signal:

  • Subscription products with variable renewal windows
  • E-commerce platforms with irregular repurchase cycles
  • Financial services with long-gap but high-value transactions
  • SaaS platforms where usage and billing are separate signals
  • Any platform with a meaningful gap between purchase intent and purchase action

The framework's fundamental insight — that activity between purchases is as important as the purchases themselves — transfers across all of these contexts.

B
Behind the Build

How this analysis was built

This case study was produced entirely from raw transactional data. No third-party retention tools were used. The methodology was designed from first principles — starting with a question rather than a template.

Analytical Approach

Data Source

Raw purchase transaction log — 581,000 users, multiple years of history. No pre-aggregated summaries. Every row = one purchase event.

Primary Tool

SQL — window functions for sequential purchase ordering, LAG() for inter-purchase gap calculation, CASE logic for lifecycle tagging.

Segmentation Logic

31-day gap threshold derived empirically — tested at 14, 21, 31, 45 days. 31 days produced the cleanest behavioural split between natural gaps and disengagement.

Validation

Each segment's return rate was back-tested across 5 historical cohorts. Reacquisition rates of 42–50% in later cycles confirmed the model's consistency.
