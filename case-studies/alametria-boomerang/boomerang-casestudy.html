01 · The Challenge
Churn Was a One-Way Door

The Story of Sarah

Imagine a customer who signed up last March. She made her first purchase, traded actively for about six weeks, and then — silence. No logins, no trades, no purchases for 45 days. In the old model, Sarah was marked as "churned" and moved to a suppression list. Lost cause. Campaign budget wasted on her.

But here's what the data showed: Sarah came back. Two months later, she logged in, made another purchase, and started trading again. She wasn't gone — she was taking a break. And Sarah wasn't alone. Roughly 1 in 3 customers who "drop off" eventually return. We call them Boomerang customers.

The problem wasn't that Sarah left. The problem was that we had no system to predict her drop, no tailored campaign to bring her back at the right moment, and no way to recognise her return as a meaningful behavioral event rather than a random fluke.

The core failure: Every drop-off looked identical. A customer who'd been inactive for 2 days and one who'd been gone for 6 months received the same treatment — because we had no mechanism to tell them apart or act differently.

Four Things That Were Broken

"Gone Forever" Assumption

Every inactive customer was marked churned and removed from active segments. No recovery model. 130,000 returns were happening invisibly — with zero credit going to any campaign.

Same Message, Every Segment

A 2-day dropout and a 6-month dropout received identical win-back emails. Timing was ignored. Cycle history was ignored. Context was ignored entirely.

No Early Warning System

Drop-offs were only visible after the 31-day mark. By the time the data showed a customer had dropped, the most effective intervention window had already closed.

Revenue Left Unmeasured

With no lifecycle tracking, nobody knew that returning customers were already generating 30–35% of total annual revenue. That revenue had no source attribution — it was invisible.

02 · Methodology
How the Pipeline Works

Boomerang_v1 pulls from three data sources and merges them into a single unified timeline per customer. The pipeline runs daily, so the output table is always current.

The Six-Step Pipeline

Step 1 — Clean the Data

Standardise all email addresses (lowercase, trim whitespace). Keep only valid transactions from approved payment methods. Remove test accounts and policy-flagged customers.

Step 2 — Find Each Customer's Starting Point

For every customer: identify their first purchase date (their "birthday" on the platform), which coupon brought them in, and what country they're from. This anchors the entire lifecycle.

Step 3 — Build a Unified Activity Timeline

Combine purchase dates and trading activity days into a single chronological timeline. This gives a complete picture of when the customer was genuinely active — not just when they transacted.

Step 4 — Detect Gaps of 31+ Days

Scan the timeline for consecutive periods of 31 or more days with no activity. Each such gap is one Drop event. The 31-day threshold was empirically derived — long enough to confirm real disengagement, short enough to act before the recovery window closes.

Step 5 — Classify Each Drop by LTD Bucket

For each Drop event, calculate how long the customer was active before the gap began. This "Lifetime to Drop" (LTD) value determines which bucket the customer falls into:

Why LTD matters: The bucket tells us who the customer was before they left — not just that they left. An LTD300d+ dropout receives a completely different win-back approach, message, and offer than an LTD45d who never fully onboarded.

Step 6 — Track Returns and Open New Cycles

When a customer re-engages after a Drop (makes a purchase or resumes trading), a Return event is logged and a new cycle opens. Each cycle tracks its own LTD bucket, days active, days inactive, revenue, withdrawals, and purchase count. Up to 5 complete cycles are tracked per customer.

03 · The Framework
Drop + Return + Escape

Every customer at any point in time exists in one of three states. The Boomerang framework tracks movement between them continuously.

State · Drop
The Gap
31+ consecutive days with no purchase or trading activity. Customer enters the recovery window. Not gone — just absent. A Drop is not a verdict.
State · Return
The Boomerang
Customer re-engages after a Drop. Makes a purchase or resumes trading. Every return opens a new cycle with its own complete data record.
State · Escape
True Churn
Extended absence of 365+ days with no return signal. Tracked separately from active drops to avoid campaign spend on customers who are genuinely gone.

Key principle: Boomerang_v1 does not label a customer as "churned" permanently. Every Drop is a state — not a verdict. The distinction between Drop and Escape is what enables targeted win-back campaigns without wasted spend.

5-Cycle Tracking

Customers don't just drop once. Some drop and return multiple times. Boomerang tracks up to 5 complete cycles per customer, with each cycle storing independent metrics. A "Super Boomerang" customer who completes five cycles is not a churn risk — they are demonstrating the most consistent loyalty pattern on the platform.

04 · The Critical Insight
Return Rates Get Better, Not Worse

Most churn frameworks assume that the longer a customer has been gone, the less likely they are to come back. And that customers who have churned once are inherently less reliable than customers who haven't. Boomerang data shows the opposite on both counts.

05 · Key Findings
Four Things We Didn't Know Before

Finding 1 — The 45-Day Critical Window

The largest single LTD bucket is LTD45d. Customers who drop before Day 45 never fully onboarded — they made a purchase (or a few), encountered friction, and left before establishing a rhythm. They are the most numerous group, the hardest to win back (low engagement history means low platform attachment), and — crucially — the most preventable.

If a customer is still active at Day 45, their likelihood of becoming a long-term retained user increases significantly. The first six weeks are make-or-break. Interventions at Day 14, 21, and 30 target this window before the drop occurs — not after.

Finding 2 — Returning Customers Are Not Higher Risk

The withdrawal-to-revenue ratio holds steady at 52–54% across all lifecycle stages — including customers on their 4th or 5th return cycle. A returning customer behaves financially identically to a new customer. This finding directly eliminates the business justification for applying premium restrictions or reduced offers to win-back customers.

Policy implication: There is no financial risk argument for treating returning customers differently from new customers. Removing offer restrictions for this segment unlocks a conversion improvement with no corresponding increase in withdrawal exposure.

Finding 3 — The Revenue Reality

30–35% of total annual revenue already comes from returning customers. This was entirely invisible before Boomerang. Every campaign budget that ignored returnees was leaving a third of revenue unattributed — and therefore unoptimised. The platform's retention performance was being systematically underreported.

Finding 4 — Promo Codes Drive Real Returns

Approximately 42% of returning customers used a promo code for their comeback purchase. Win-back campaigns work — but only when the campaign reaches the right customer at the right timing relative to their LTD bucket, not when sent as a blanket email to every inactive address on a list.

08 · Scalability
Built Once. Grows With the Business.

The Boomerang_v1 framework is not a one-time analysis. It is a living system designed to scale as the business grows and as the data matures.

  • Applicable to any product line with repeat-purchase or repeat-engagement behaviour — not limited to the current challenge account model
  • Cycle count can extend beyond 5 as the customer base matures and multi-return behaviour becomes more common
  • LTD buckets can be recalibrated if the empirical 31-day threshold shifts as product cadence changes
  • Propensity scoring can be layered on top — using cycle 1 LTD bucket and activity patterns to predict future drops before they happen
  • Regional segmentation (country-level LTD analysis) can be added as a second dimension once the base model is fully embedded

B · Behind the Build
Methodology in Detail

Why 31 Days?

The 31-day inactivity threshold was chosen empirically based on Alametria's transaction rhythm. It is long enough to distinguish a genuine disengagement from a normal gap between trading activity — customers who transact monthly will not be incorrectly flagged. It is short enough to identify the recovery window before it closes: data shows that win-back campaigns sent at Day 35 significantly outperform those sent at Day 60 or 90, and campaigns beyond Day 90 show sharply diminishing returns for most LTD buckets.

How the Timeline Is Constructed

Three data types are merged into a single daily activity marker per customer: purchase transaction dates, trading activity days (any day where the customer placed or managed a trade), and the first purchase date as a lifecycle anchor. A customer is considered "active" on any day where at least one of these three signals is present. The timeline is then scanned chronologically for gaps.

How Gaps Are Detected

For each customer, the pipeline walks through the activity timeline day by day. When a gap of 31 or more consecutive inactive days is found, a Drop event is recorded. The LTD is calculated as the number of active days between the previous Return (or the first purchase date for cycle 1) and the first day of the gap. The gap length (days inactive) is calculated from the first inactive day to the first active day of the subsequent Return — or to the current date if no Return has occurred.

Edge Cases

If a customer shows activity on exactly Day 31 of a gap, they are not flagged as a Drop — the threshold requires 31 full consecutive days. If a customer purchases on a day they were not trading, that purchase day counts as an active day. Customers on the policy violation list are included in the data table but flagged so marketing can exclude them from all outreach.

Data Quality Decisions

Test accounts (identifiable by internal email patterns) are removed before the pipeline runs. Transactions from unsupported payment methods are excluded. Email addresses are standardised to lowercase with leading and trailing whitespace removed before deduplication, preventing the same customer from appearing twice under slightly different email formats.
